# Day 33 – Media Integrity Analyzer

## Overview

Today I built **Media Integrity Analyzer**, an interactive educational web application that teaches users how to critically evaluate digital information before believing or sharing it.

The simulator guides users through analyzing fictional headlines and emotionally charged content, helping them recognize misleading language, emotional manipulation, and bias through interactive discovery rather than memorization.

The project is built as a **single offline HTML file** using **HTML, CSS, and Vanilla JavaScript** with no external libraries or APIs.

---

---

# Interactive Learning Flow

The simulator teaches media literacy step by step by explaining each concept before presenting a challenge.

Each lesson answers:

- What is this?
- Why does it matter?
- How does it affect everyday life?

---

# Challenge 1 – Headline Detective

A fictional news headline and matching article are generated.

Users are asked to:

- Decide whether they would click the headline.
- Identify exaggerated or misleading phrases.
- Think critically before revealing the analysis.

After submission, the simulator displays:

- Headline Accuracy Score
- Highlighted misleading phrases
- Explanation of inaccuracies
- Fair rewritten headline
- Key learning takeaway

---

# Challenge 2 – Emotion Detector

A fictional social media post, article excerpt, or reel caption is presented.

Users identify:

- Their emotional reaction
- Words that influenced those emotions

The simulator then reveals:

- Intended audience
- Emotional manipulation technique
- Highlighted emotional trigger words
- Neutral rewritten version
- Key takeaway for critical thinking

---

# Live Media Integrity Metrics

During the simulation, four live metrics update dynamically based on user interactions:

- Headline Accuracy
- Source Reliability
- Emotional Manipulation
- Audience Targeting

These metrics help users understand how different aspects contribute to media credibility.

---

# Final Media Integrity Dashboard

At the end of the experience, users receive a comprehensive report including:

- Overall Media Integrity Score
- What the user learned
- Biggest red flag detected
- Three practical media literacy habits
- Replay option with completely new scenarios

# Key Learnings

- Headlines are often written to capture attention and may exaggerate facts.
- Emotional language can influence opinions before evidence is evaluated.
- Critical thinking requires reading beyond the headline and considering context.
- Recognizing manipulation techniques helps prevent misinformation from spreading.
- Evaluating credibility involves examining language, intent, and source reliability together.
- Practicing media literacy encourages informed decision-making in everyday digital life.
