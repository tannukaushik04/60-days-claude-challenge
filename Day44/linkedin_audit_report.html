Profile report card — before

Headline

3/10

Recruiter's 3-second read: "another student who listed every buzzword they could fit."

"Data Science & AI Enthusiast | Artificial intelligence | Machine Learning | Data Analytics | Software Developer | NPTEL Certified"

Why it hurtsYou've listed AI, Machine Learning, and Data Analytics as if they're three different things — to a recruiter they read as one repeated keyword, not three skills. "Enthusiast" signals hobbyist, not builder. Cramming in "Software Developer" too tells recruiters you don't know what role you're going for, so they can't tell if you fit their req.

Invisible costLinkedIn search ranks headlines by relevance to a specific role. A headline that tries to match everything matches nothing well — you're likely buried below candidates with one sharp, specific headline.

About — first 2 lines (pre "see more")

2/10

This is the only part of your About that shows without a click. Recruiter's read: "generic student intro, next profile."

"I am a passionate and motivated Computer Science student with a strong interest in Data Science, Artificial Intelligence, and Python development."

Why it hurts"Passionate and motivated" is the single most common opening phrase on LinkedIn — recruiters mentally skip it. It says how you feel about the field, not what you've built or can do. There's no hook, no reason to click "see more."

Invisible costMost recruiters decide whether to keep reading within these two lines. If they don't click "see more," they never see your projects, your skills, or your NPTEL cert — the entire rest of your profile might as well not exist.

About — full section

3/10

"Sounds like every other CS student's About section — nothing here differentiates them."

Why it hurtsThe whole section is intent, not evidence — "I enjoy learning," "continuously exploring," "grow as a future professional." No project is named, no result is quantified, and there's no call to action telling a reader what to do next (message you? check your projects?).

Invisible costYou have two real, working full-stack projects. A recruiter reading your About has no idea they exist — you're hiding your strongest proof behind vague aspiration language.

Experience

0/10

"No experience section at all — is this profile even active?"

Why it hurtsYou don't have formal work experience yet, which is normal for a student — but LinkedIn's Experience section isn't only for jobs. You can (and should) list projects there with dates, description, and tech stack. Right now that section is empty, so the profile reads as unfinished.

Invisible costRecruiters searching by tech stack (Flask, MySQL, Python) will never find you, because that content isn't sitting anywhere it gets indexed. HerHealth AI and your Cafe Management System are currently invisible to search.

Skills & keywords

4/10

"Fine, but thin — this profile won't surface for half the searches a recruiter runs in this space."

HTML, CSS, JS, Python, Machine Learning, AI, Data Science, Data Structures

Why it hurtsYou've actually used Flask, MySQL, and built AI chatbot integrations and report-generation pipelines — none of that is reflected in your Skills list. "Data Structures" is a coursework topic, not a hireable skill keyword; it dilutes the list rather than strengthening it.

Invisible costLinkedIn's recruiter search is largely skill-keyword driven. Every real skill you don't list is a search query you don't show up in — including searches for exactly the tools you actually know.

Option 2 · Value-proposition (for hiring managers who scan fast)
I build AI-powered apps that solve real problems | Data Science & Python developer | Open to full-time roles
Use this on your homepage/DMs stage — it states outcome over title, which reads stronger to a hiring manager scrolling fast.
Option 3 · Authority style (for network growth & visibility)
Aspiring Data Scientist | Turning data into health & business insights | Python · ML · Flask | NPTEL Certified
Use this if you're posting content and want to be seen as building a track record, not just job-hunting.

Since your goal is both landing a job and growing your network, start with Option 1 now (search visibility matters most while applying), and switch to Option 3 once you start posting regularly.

About section — rewrite

The hook (before "see more")
Most student projects stop at a working demo. Mine ship as full platforms people could actually use.
Why: replaces a feelings-statement with a specific claim that earns a click.
The story
I'm a Computer Science student focused on Data Science and AI, and I learn by building — not just studying theory. I work end-to-end: designing the database, writing the backend logic in Flask, and wiring up the AI layer that makes the app actually useful, not just functional.
Why: "human language, not corporate speak" — this explains what you do day-to-day, in plain terms a recruiter can picture.
Proof
Two projects show this in practice: HerHealth AI, a full-stack women's health platform with an integrated AI chatbot and automated health-report generation — and a Cafe Management System that unifies menu, orders, billing, and staff management into one tool. Both are built on Python, Flask, and MySQL. I'm also NPTEL certified in the space.
Why: names the actual proof that was buried before — recruiters now see evidence, not adjectives.
CTA
Open to data science, AI, and Python development roles — message me, or check the Featured section below for a closer look at both projects.
Why: tells the reader exactly what to do next instead of leaving the About section as a dead end.

Keywords embedded — Data Science, AI, Python, Flask, MySQL, full-stack, AI chatbot, healthcare platform, NPTEL certified.

7-day LinkedIn activation plan

Day 1 — make all profile changes +
  • Swap headline to Option 1 (keyword-optimized)
  • Replace About section with the rewritten hook + story + proof + CTA
  • Add both projects to the Experience section with dates and the rewritten bullets
  • Update Skills: add the 10 new keywords, remove "Data Structures," pin Python / Machine Learning / Flask
  • Upload both project links or screenshots to Featured
  • Turn on "Open to Work" (recruiters only, if you'd rather it stay private)
Day 2 — publish post #1 +

Post title: "I just rebuilt my LinkedIn profile"

I just rebuilt my LinkedIn profile from scratch — and it made me realize how much of it was empty adjectives instead of actual proof. Before: "passionate CS student interested in Data Science and AI." After: two real projects, listed with the tech stack and what they actually do. One of them, HerHealth AI, tracks menstrual health and generates personalized reports using an integrated AI chatbot. The other, a Cafe Management System, runs orders, billing, and staff management in one dashboard. Lesson: recruiters don't hire enthusiasm. They hire evidence. Open to Data Science / AI / Python roles — always happy to connect with people building in this space.
Day 3 — send 10 strategic connection requests +

Who to target

  • Recruiters at 3-5 companies you'd actually want to work at (search "technical recruiter" + company name)
  • Data scientists / ML engineers 1-3 years ahead of you — they remember the junior search and often reply
  • Alumni from your college now working in Data Science or AI roles

Connection message template

Hi [Name], I'm a CS student building AI/data projects (like an AI-powered healthcare platform). Would love to connect and follow your work in [their field].
Day 4 — comment on 5 posts in your target space +

The value comment formula

  • Agree — one line showing you actually read the post
  • Add insight — one specific thing from your own experience or project that relates
  • Ask a question — keeps the conversation open and often gets a reply from the original poster

Example: "Agree that model accuracy alone is misleading — I ran into this building a health-report generator, where a technically 'accurate' report still felt useless to the user. Curious how you'd measure real-world usefulness instead of just accuracy?"

Day 5 — publish post #2 +

Post title: a lesson from building HerHealth AI

Building an AI chatbot into HerHealth AI taught me something no course did: the hard part isn't the model, it's making the output useful. Anyone can call an API and get a response. Turning that response into a personalized, readable health report someone will actually trust — that took more iteration than the AI integration itself. Hot take: for most student AI projects, the "AI" part is 20% of the actual work. The other 80% is UX, data structure, and knowing what the user actually needs to see. What's the most underrated part of your projects that nobody asks about?
Day 6 — engage + send 5 more requests +
  • Reply to every comment on both posts within a few hours — replies drive LinkedIn's algorithm more than the post itself
  • Send 5 more connection requests using the same template from Day 3, targeting a different company or niche this time
Day 7 — review the numbers +

What to check

  • Profile views — a jump here means your headline/photo are earning clicks from search or posts
  • Connection acceptance rate — under 30% usually means your message or target audience needs adjusting, not more volume
  • Post impressions and comments — comments matter more than likes; they're what the algorithm rewards

If views are up but acceptance is low, tighten your connection message to be more specific to each person. If both are flat, the issue is usually the headline, not the outreach.
